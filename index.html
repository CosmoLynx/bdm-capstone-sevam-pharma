The Business

Meet Sevam Pharma — The Underdog of Medicine Distribution

Sevam Pharma LLP is not some giant multinational. It's a lean, B2B pharmaceutical distributor tucked in Sangli, Maharashtra, supplying medicines — mostly from Sun Pharma — to retail medical stores in the region. Think of them as the invisible backbone between the medicine factory and your local pharmacy.

Founded in 2022, they had 12 employees, a warehouse full of boxes, and a growing list of problems that come with growth: too much of some medicines, not enough of others, and retailers returning stock left and right.

"The warehouse was running on gut feel and old habits. My job was to replace that with data — and make the owner actually trust it."

Getting the data wasn't glamorous. It meant having real conversations with the owner, getting an official authorization letter signed, and sitting through the slightly terrifying experience of explaining what "data analysis" means to someone who's been doing business without it for years. But they said yes — and that was everything.

The Problem

Four Problems That Were Quietly Draining Money

Before touching any code, I spent time understanding the business. Four core problem statements emerged:

💊
Inventory & Expiry Chaos

Slow-moving medicines rotting on shelves = money literally expiring. Fast-moving ones running out = missed sales. Both happening simultaneously — a classic lose-lose.

🏪
Underperforming Retail Outlets

Some retailers were receiving stock but returning a shocking 25–35% of it. Why? Nobody had stopped to find out. Until now.

📈
Seasonal Demand Blindspot

Procurement was based on habit, not data. February demand ≠ October demand. Nobody had mapped this out clearly.

⚠️
Data Anomalies & Inconsistencies

Stock records that violated the formula: Closing = Opening + Purchase − Sales. Missing material codes. Same medicine, different names. The dataset was a puzzle.

The Toolkit

The Arsenal — What I Actually Did With the Data

Once the data was cleaned (which took longer than I'd like to admit), I deployed a mix of inventory management techniques and statistical analysis. Here's what I used and why:

Inventory

ABC Analysis

Classified all medicines by revenue contribution. Turned out 9 products = 80% of revenue. Classic Pareto in action.

Inventory

FSN Analysis

Split medicines into Fast, Slow, Non-moving. 33 fast movers, 11 slow, 7 non-moving. Non-movers = dead capital.

Risk

Expiry Risk Scoring

Built a custom formula: Months of Stock ÷ Months to Expiry. Score > 1 = danger zone. 21.5% of products were high-risk.

Risk

Inventory Turnover

Sales ÷ Average Inventory. Low ratio = stock sitting idle. Flagged the worst offenders for immediate action.

Retail

Retailer Contribution Analysis

Mapped which retailers actually drive revenue vs which ones just return stock. One retailer = 39% of revenue. Mind-blowing.

Retail

Return Impact Analysis

Quantified how much returned stock was eating into realized sales. The numbers were uncomfortable to present.

Trends

Demand & Trend Analysis

Mapped demand month by month. February = peak. April = valley. October = tricky — high demand, lower margins. A seasonal story emerged.

Anomalies

Correlation + Anomaly Detection

Opening-Closing stock correlation: 0.90. Sales-Closing stock: −0.71. Found records where stock math simply didn't add up.

The Jaw-Dropping Findings

What the Data Actually Revealed

Here's where things got genuinely interesting. I expected to find some inventory issues. I didn't expect the data to tell such a clear, almost dramatic story.

The 9-Product Empire

Just 9 medicines out of 51 were responsible for 80% of total revenue. The top product alone — PRAMIPEX ER 0.375 TA — contributed nearly 39% of all revenue by itself. The business was essentially built on a handful of medicines. One supply disruption and the whole thing wobbles.

The Retailer Who Runs Everything

A single retailer called Pooja Palus was generating 39.2% of all revenue. The next biggest? 14.1%. Most others? A rounding error labeled "Others." But here's the twist — when I looked at sales quantity, the distribution was much more even. It's not that Pooja Palus buys more medicines; it's that they buy more expensive ones. Fascinating.

# The inventory math that kept breaking
Rule: Closing = Opening + Purchase – Sales
Reality: Several records: NOPE. Just... nope.

Returns Are a Silent Killer

Several retailers were returning 350 to 450 units out of every 1,100–1,400 units supplied. That's 25–35% of stock coming back unrealized. The business was shipping more than retailers could actually sell — a textbook mismatch between supply push and actual demand pull.

High Sales ≠ High Profit (Surprise!)

February had the highest revenue. But March and May had larger profit bubbles. October saw rising demand paired with shrinking margins. The relationship between volume and profitability was more complicated than anyone realized — and now there's data to prove it.

Takeaways

What I Told the Business to Do Differently

Data without recommendations is just decoration. Here's what I proposed — grounded in the findings:

Adopt a sales-based replenishment strategy — buy 1.2–1.5× the average of the last 2–3 months, not based on gut feel. Set clear reorder levels per product so stockouts become an alert, not a surprise. For overstocked items? Discount them, redistribute them, or pause procurement. For the high-return retailers? Set maximum supply limits based on their actual consumption history.

The big one: move from uniform distribution (sending the same stock to every retailer) to retailer-specific allocation based on historical sales performance. Not every pharmacy is the same. The data makes that embarrassingly clear.

"The data wasn't broken. The decisions being made without it — those were broken."
